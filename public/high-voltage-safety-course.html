High voltage electricity is a powerful force that enables modern life, but it also presents unique and severe dangers. By understanding these risks, you'll be better prepared to protect yourself and others, forming the foundation for all safe work practices covered in this course.

Why High Voltage is Uniquely Hazardous

High voltage electricity poses significantly greater dangers compared to standard electrical systems. The hazards are often not immediately apparent — risks can be invisible and silent. Even brief contact can result in severe injury or death.

The Main Hazards

Expand each section to learn about the primary hazards. Each includes a real-world example.

High voltage can cause rapid heating of skin and deep tissue, resulting in severe burns both on the surface and inside the body. Even brief exposure can lead to second-degree burns at energy levels as low as 1.2 calories per square centimeter per second.


Example: A worker accidentally contacting a live conductor may suffer deep tissue burns that require extensive medical treatment.

An arc flash is a sudden, explosive release of energy between conductive objects. This can create a powerful blast wave, intense heat, and airborne shrapnel, all of which pose serious risks to anyone nearby.


Example: A tool dropped near energised equipment can trigger an arc flash, causing burns and injuries to multiple workers in the vicinity.

High voltage can trigger rapid, involuntary muscle contractions, making it impossible to release a live conductor once contact is made. This increases the duration and severity of the shock.


Consequence: A worker becomes trapped, unable to let go, with electricity continuing to flow through their body.

High voltage incidents can ignite surrounding materials, cause explosions, and result in major damage to equipment and infrastructure. The thermal energy released can set clothing and nearby objects on fire.


Example: An electrical fault in a high voltage panel may lead to a fire that spreads rapidly throughout a facility.

High voltage can arc through air, creating a dangerous electrical bridge between the source and a person or object nearby. This means you can be injured without ever directly touching a live conductor.


Example: A worker standing too close to energised equipment may be injured by an arc that jumps the gap, even without physical contact.

Fundamental Safety Principles

Understanding and applying these key principles is essential for anyone working with high voltage systems.

Always treat every circuit as if it is energised until you have verified otherwise. This mindset prevents accidental contact with live parts.
Wear insulated gloves, dielectric boots, face shields, and flame-resistant clothing designed for high voltage environments. The correct PPE can mean the difference between a close call and a serious injury.
Ensure all equipment is de-energised, locked, and tagged before starting any work. This process protects you and your team from unexpected energisation.
High voltage can arc through air, so always keep the minimum safe approach distance based on voltage levels. Never assume you are safe just because you are not touching the equipment.

Workplace Preparation & DNOT Procedures

Before any high voltage work begins, thorough preparation and strict adherence to procedures are non-negotiable. Building on your understanding of high voltage hazards, you'll now learn the practical steps that keep you and your team safe before the work even starts.

The Three Pillars of Workplace Preparation

Explore the foundational elements that must be in place before any high voltage work begins.

Only those who are properly trained, authorised, and competent should perform high voltage work. This includes having up-to-date site inductions and being able to demonstrate knowledge of electrical safety rules, rescue, and resuscitation procedures.


If you are not authorised, you must always be accompanied and supervised by someone who is.

Before starting any job, a thorough job safety analysis must be completed with your entire work party. All tools and PPE should be inspected to ensure they are fit for use.


Testing devices must be calibrated and functioning, and warning signs and barriers need to be in place and clearly visible.

High voltage work is never a solo task. Only authorised personnel should participate, and anyone not fully authorised must be supervised at all times.


Team communication and readiness are critical to ensure everyone understands their role and the safety procedures in place.

DNOT Procedures: Your Last Line of Defence

Do Not Operate Tag (DNOT) procedures are your critical safeguard against accidental energisation. Strictly following each step in the correct order is essential — there is no room for shortcuts when safety is on the line.

De-energise
Turn off and isolate all sources of electrical energy. Verify that all switches and disconnects are in the off position and that no power is present.
Lock & Tag
Attach locks and Do Not Operate tags to all switches and disconnects to prevent accidental re-energisation while work is in progress.
Test
Use approved testing devices to confirm that no voltage is present. Always test after de-energising and locking/tagging, never before.
Begin Work
Only after all previous steps are completed and confirmed should work begin. Skipping or reordering steps can result in serious injury or death.
What Can Go Wrong?

Explore the risks associated with skipping any step in the DNOT procedure.

If you fail to de-energise equipment, live voltage may still be present, putting anyone who touches it at immediate risk of severe shock or electrocution. Even a momentary lapse can result in life-threatening injuries.
Without locks and tags, someone else could unknowingly re-energise the equipment while you are working on it. This can cause sudden, unexpected energisation and catastrophic injury.
Assuming equipment is safe without testing can lead to a "false safe" situation, where hidden voltage remains present. This step is your final confirmation that it is truly safe to begin work.

Module 03
Hierarchy of Controls & PPE Selection

In this lesson, you'll learn how electrical risks are managed using a structured approach, and why personal protective equipment (PPE) is considered the last line of defence. Understanding and applying these concepts is essential for keeping yourself and your team safe.

Learning Objectives
  • Explain the hierarchy of controls for electrical safety.
  • Describe the role and limitations of PPE.
  • Apply the five-step process for selecting PPE.
  • Identify the minimum PPE requirements for high voltage work.
  • Match PPE items to the hazards they protect against.
The Hierarchy of Controls

Each level of the hierarchy plays a specific role in reducing danger, with PPE always being the last resort.

1. Elimination
Completely removing the electrical hazard from the workplace. Most effective control, but not always possible. Example: decommissioning unused high voltage equipment.
2. Substitution
Replacing a hazardous process or equipment with something less dangerous, such as using lower voltage equipment where possible.
3. Isolation
Physically separating people from the hazard using barriers, locked switchrooms, or remote switching devices.
4. Engineering Controls
Design features that minimise risk: interlocks, insulation, and automatic shutdown systems built into equipment.
5. Administrative Controls
Procedures, training, signage, and supervision. Most effective when combined with other controls.
6. Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)
The final barrier. PPE does not eliminate the hazard but provides protection if all other controls fail. Always use in conjunction with higher-level controls, never as the sole protection.
🛡️ PPE is your last line of defence — never rely on it as your only protection.
Five-Step PPE Selection Process

Choosing the right PPE is not guesswork — it requires a systematic approach.

Identify all electrical and arc hazards in your work area. Recognise sources of high voltage, potential for arc flash, and other risks. Example: working near energised switchgear presents both shock and arc flash hazards.
Determine which equipment or locations could produce an arc flash — switchgear, transformers, and circuit breakers. Not all equipment poses the same risk; prioritise areas with the highest arc hazard potential.
Calculate the incident energy or arc flash level for each identified asset, measured in calories per square centimeter. This must be done by qualified personnel. Knowing the exact energy level guides PPE selection.
Use your organisation's risk management framework to evaluate the likelihood and consequences of an arc flash. Consider work duration, proximity, and environmental conditions.
Based on the calculated hazard and risk assessment, choose PPE that meets or exceeds the required arc rating and complies with relevant standards. Always consult qualified personnel and never select PPE based on assumptions.

Key Personnel Roles

The three essential roles form the backbone of safe high voltage operations. Each role has specific qualifications, responsibilities, and limitations.

Someone with technical knowledge or sufficient experience, assessed as competent, and officially approved to carry out certain functions under safety rules. Responsible for issuing access permits, supervising work, and ensuring all safety procedures are followed. Example: overseeing the isolation and earthing of equipment before maintenance begins.
Works under the direct supervision of an Authorised Person. Trained to avoid electrical dangers but cannot work independently on high voltage systems. Example: may assist with tasks during a shutdown, but only while being closely supervised.
Specifically assigned to monitor work near electrical apparatus, warn against unsafe approaches, and provide emergency assistance if needed. Must remain vigilant and cannot be assigned other duties while observing. Example: may halt work if someone or equipment gets too close to an exclusion zone.

Module 05
Electromagnetic Induction Hazards & Controls

Working near high voltage lines involves more than just avoiding direct contact — some of the most dangerous hazards are invisible. Electromagnetic induction can create lethal voltages on equipment and conductors, even when they appear de-energised.

Learning Objectives
  • Explain electromagnetic induction hazards.
  • Distinguish electric and magnetic fields.
  • Recognise exposure limits and physiological effects.
  • Describe equipotential zone setup steps.
  • Verify induction hazard controls before work.
Understanding Electromagnetic Induction
Electromagnetic induction occurs when electric and magnetic fields from energised high voltage lines induce voltages onto nearby de-energised or non-commissioned conductors. A de-energised line running parallel to an energised line can become dangerously charged, even if not directly connected to a power source. This phenomenon is invisible but can create hazardous conditions for workers who may not expect voltage to be present.
Induced voltages can be high enough to cause severe electric shock or even death. These voltages may appear on lines or equipment that have been de-energised, creating a false sense of security. If a worker touches a conductor with induced voltage, current can flow through their body to ground, resulting in serious injury. Strict controls and awareness are essential.
Induction hazards are most common near energised and de-energised parallel lines, at stringing sites, and in environments where conductive objects run close to high voltage transmission lines. Examples: fences, pipelines, rail lines, or new lines being constructed beside live circuits.

Concept

Equipotential Field

Click to reveal →
Definition

Lines of equal voltage in the air around conductors. Understanding these helps prevent dangerous voltage differences at worksites.

Exposure Limits
Occupational exposure to electric fields is limited to 10 kV/m, while the general public limit is 5 kV/m. Near energised conductors, electric field strengths can reach up to 16 kV/m at the surface of high voltage lines. Exceeding these limits increases the risk of electric shock and other health hazards.
For magnetic fields, the occupational exposure limit is 10,000 mG, and for the general public it is 2,000 mG. For example, at 100 mm from a conductor carrying 500A, the field can reach 10,000 mG. Occupational limits are higher because workers are generally healthier and better able to withstand exposure.
Exposure to strong electric and magnetic fields can cause nerve and muscle stimulation, unpleasant skin discharges, and visual phenomena called phosphenes (flashing lights in the eyes). Understanding these effects helps workers recognise symptoms of overexposure.

Six-Step Equipotential Zone Setup

Follow these six steps to establish a safe equipotential zone at worksites where induction hazards are present. Each step is essential.

Lay earth mats around the perimeter of all machinery and equipment. Ensure there are no gaps where a worker could step off the mat onto bare ground. This provides a continuous safe surface.
Connect all mats to each other, earth stakes, and machinery using proper earthing lugs and bonding leads. This ensures all surfaces are at the same potential, reducing step and touch voltages.
Set up fencing around the inner edge of the matting to prevent accidental step-off. This physical barrier keeps workers within the protected zone.
Place a secondary fence outside the inner fence. The size and shape should be adapted to the specific site and equipment layout for added protection.
Ensure all conductive objects within the fenced area are bonded to the earth mats. This prevents hazardous voltage differences from developing on tools or equipment.
Install insulated rubber matting between the inner and outer fences at a designated entry/exit point. This allows safe access for personnel entering or leaving the zone.
⚡ Effective control of electromagnetic induction hazards is essential — thorough setup and verification of equipotential zones are critical for ensuring safety and saving lives.

Emergency Response & Incident Procedures

Electrical emergencies can happen even in the safest workplaces. Knowing how to respond quickly and correctly is just as important as preventing incidents in the first place. Being prepared for emergencies can save lives and minimise harm — your response matters.

Learning Objectives

  • Respond appropriately to mobile plant electrical contact incidents.
  • Understand and execute incident reporting and site preservation.
  • Recall critical actions in electrical emergencies.
Prerequisites for Safe Rescue Operations

Before attempting any rescue in an electrical emergency, certain safety conditions must be met.

All conductors must be isolated, proved de-energised, and earthed at every source by an Authorised Person before anyone attempts a rescue within safe approach distances. Skipping this step puts rescuers at extreme risk of becoming victims themselves. Never assume a conductor is safe — always follow the full isolation and earthing procedure.
After verifying that the area is de-energised, working earths must be applied at the location. This ensures that any unexpected re-energisation or residual voltage is safely diverted, further protecting everyone involved.
Once the immediate emergency is under control, standard safety procedures must be restored as soon as possible. This includes proper documentation, incident investigation, and returning to established safety protocols.

Emergency Response for Mobile Plant Contact
If you are on the vehicle, remain there unless it is unsafe to do so. The vehicle may be energised, and stepping off could complete a circuit to ground, resulting in severe injury or death.
Alert everyone nearby to stay clear of the vehicle and surrounding area. The ground around the vehicle may be energised, creating step and touch potential hazards for anyone approaching.
No one should attempt to rescue or approach the vehicle until the electrical lines and equipment have been isolated and earthed by qualified personnel.
If you must leave the vehicle due to immediate danger (such as fire), jump well clear without touching the vehicle and ground at the same time. Land with your feet together and shuffle away without lifting your feet to avoid step potential.

Incident Reporting and Site Preservation
All incidents, no matter how minor, must be reported to your immediate supervisor as soon as possible. Prompt reporting ensures that hazards are addressed and helps prevent future incidents. Failing to report can allow dangerous conditions to go unnoticed and uncorrected.
Only take actions necessary to prevent further harm or damage. Leave the scene undisturbed so investigators can determine the cause and prevent recurrence. Disturbing the site can destroy evidence and hinder the investigation process.
Incident investigations identify root causes and help improve safety procedures. Your accurate reporting and site preservation contribute to a safer workplace for everyone.
🚨 In electrical emergencies, every second counts — your knowledge and disciplined response protect both lives and livelihoods.

Key Organisations and Standards
A global organisation that develops international standards for electrical, electronic, and related technologies. IEC standards define voltage thresholds and testing procedures used in high voltage operations. Following IEC standards ensures equipment and procedures meet internationally recognised safety levels.
The primary regulatory body for workplace safety in the United States. OSHA standards are legally enforceable and establish minimum safety requirements for employers and workers, including those related to electrical hazards. OSHA mandates lockout/tagout procedures and safe approach distances for high voltage work. Compliance is mandatory for all U.S. workplaces.
A standard focused specifically on electrical safety in the workplace. Provides detailed guidance on safe work practices, selection of PPE, and arc flash hazard assessment. NFPA 70E outlines how to determine the correct PPE for various electrical tasks and how to assess arc flash boundaries. Adhering to NFPA 70E helps organisations minimise the risk of electrical injuries.
